Beginnings

What is exactly "Floriography"?

Floriography, or language of flowers is a cryptic communication using flowers and their arrangement.
Plants and flowers has been used symbolically for thousands of years, starting from the Hebrew Bible
(buttercup, poppy, lavender, rockrose, violet, crocus), later famous poets and writers also used it,
like William Shakespeare (1564-1616, the most known one in Hamlet, where Ophelia has her "talking" flowers).

The start of popularity

Source: Tate Museum
Ophelia by Sir John Everett Millais Bt

Floriography and its craze was introduced to Europe by 2 people: Mary Wortley Montagu, who brought it to England (1717)
and Aubry de La Mottraye who introduced it to the Swedish court (1727).
The first publication about was Joseph Hammer-Purgstall’s Dictionnaire du languages des fleurs in 1809,
and the first dictionary was published 10 years later, in 1819 by Madame Charlotte de la Tour and was called Le langage des Fleurs.

Source: Barnebys.com
Le Langage des Fleurs by Audot

Popular periods

  • France: 1810-1850
  • England: (1820-1880, the Victorian era)
  • USA: 1830-1850

The popularization in other countries were mainly stimulated by la Tour’s book,
it reached other European countries and even South America.

Other Publications

Britain

  • Henry Phillips’ Floral Emblems (1825) in Britain.
    The first that included botanical sketches of flowers next to their names and meanings.
  • Frederic Shoberl’s -The Language of Flowers, With Illustrative Poetry (1834)
  • Robert Tyas - The Sentiment of Flowers; or Language of Flora (1836)
  • Routledge – The Language of Flowers (1884) Illustrated by Kate Greenaway

USA

  • Elizabeth Wirt – Flora’s Dictionary (1829)
  • Dorothea Dix – The Garland of Flora (1829)
  • Catharine H. Waterman Esling – Flora’s Lexicon (1839)
  • Lucy Hooper – The Lady’s Book of Flowers and Poetry (1841)
  • Frances Sargent Osgood – The Poetry of Flowers and Flowers of Poetry (1841)
  • Sarah Carter Edgarton Mayo – The Flower Vase (1844)
  • Caroline Matilda Kirkland – Poetry of Flowers (1848)

These publications were the most famous ones, the books that contained everything, from illustrations to poems.
Next to them they published magazines about flowers and poetry, sometimes the two themes together,
sometimes separately. These articles in some cases got included in the new editions of these books,
or like in the case of Kirkland, as she was the editor of two Magazine, she wrote Poetry of Flowers
later in her life, collecting everything she saw and wrote during her working days with these magazines.
This helped her book to reach 522 pages, which is one of the most comprehensive books about flowers and floriography.
